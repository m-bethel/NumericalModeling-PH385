1. Statement of Objective
The objective of this project is to simulate and analyze the transient flow of an incompressible fluid around a cylindrical bluff body using the OpenFOAM C++ framework. The simulation will specifically target the phenomenon of Von Kármán Vortex Shedding to calculate the Strouhal number (St) and analyze the periodic lift and drag forces acting on the cylinder.
2. Motivation
The Von Kármán Vortex Street is a fundamental concept in fluid-structure interaction, explaining why bridges oscillate and power lines "hum" in the wind. Using OpenFOAM allows for the study of high-Reynolds number flows that are analytically intractable, providing experience with the industry-standard software used for aerospace and civil engineering. 3. Overview of Physical Principles
The simulation is governed by the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations:
Momentum: ∂t∂u​+(u⋅∇)u=−ρ1​∇p+ν∇2u
Continuity: ∇⋅u=0
Dimensionless Parameters: The flow behavior is determined by the Reynolds Number (Re=νUD​). Vortex shedding will be triggered by targeting a Re range between 100 and 1000.
Shedding Frequency: The periodic nature of the wake is characterized by the Strouhal Number: St=UfD​, where f is the shedding frequency and D is the cylinder diameter.
4. Overview of Numerical Methods
OpenFOAM’s Finite Volume Method (FVM) will be utilized with the following configuration:
Solver: pisoFoam, a pressure-implicit with splitting of operators (PISO) algorithm designed for transient, incompressible flow.
Meshing: A 2D structured mesh generated via blockMesh, with local refinement (grading) near the cylinder surface to resolve the boundary layer and wake.
Temporal Discretization: Second-order backward Euler or Crank-Nicolson schemes to ensure temporal accuracy of the oscillating wake.
Boundary Conditions: Uniform velocity inlet, zero-gradient pressure outlet, and no-slip walls on the cylinder.
5. Development/Validation Roadmap
Phase 1 (Mesh Generation): Construct a computational domain where the cylinder is placed sufficiently far from boundaries to avoid artificial interference.
Phase 2 (Steady-State Initialization): Run a short simpleFoam simulation to establish an initial flow field.
Phase 3 (Transient Execution): Execute pisoFoam with a Courant number (Co<1) to capture the onset of instability and the subsequent periodic vortex shedding.
Phase 4 (Validation): Extract the lift coefficient (CL​) time-history. Perform a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) on the CL​ data to find the dominant frequency (f) and verify that the resulting St matches experimental values for the chosen Re.
Phase 5 (Visualization): Use ParaView to generate contour plots of Vorticity and Q-criterion to visually demonstrate the alternating vortex pairs.
